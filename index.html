FAQs

1. Why do pianos go out of tune?

There are different factors which affect tuning. These include temperature changes and changes in humidity, but the fact is that the strings are constantly exerting a force on the tuning pins which are held in a wooden plank. Whether the piano is played or not it will gradually be out of tune. It is wise to have a piano tuned at least once a year – even if it is rarely played. Keeping a piano in good condition will avoid the need for costly repairs later, or even the piano deteriorating to a degree where it is not worth fixing.

2. Does my piano need tuning after moving it?

Yes it does. Physically moving a piano will cause changes within which will affect the tuning. In addition it should be given time to settle for a period of about 2 weeks before it is tuned to help the tuning hold.

3. Is it possible to tune my piano myself?

No. Most people who try generally end up snapping strings which can be expensive and painful. It also takes years of practice to become proficient and each piano is different.

4. How often should I have my piano tuned?

A piano should be tuned at least once a year. This helps keep your piano stable (stays in tune). If it is played regularly and/or hard it may need tuning more often.

5. What precautions can I take to stop my piano from going out of tune?

Keep it somewhere where the temperature is fairly constant and not near to a heat source or in a very humid/damp atmosphere. Avoid moving it and if you do have it tuned shortly afterwards. If it has not been tuned for a long time, then when it is tuned it may go out of tune again quite quickly. It may need a couple of tunings before it settles.

6. What is a pitch raise?

Over time, if not tuned regularly, a piano will start to drop pitch and become flat. Not only will it start to sound out of tune with itself but the note A (440hz at concert pitch) will drop pitch and become flat. This is especially true if a piano has not been touched for many years. Pianos sound best at the pitch which they were designed to be and generally, the sharper they are the brighter they sound. It may take a couple of goes to get a very flat piano up to concert pitch and sometimes it’s just not possible. However as long the piano is in tune with itself and you don’t need to play with other instruments, it is fine to have a piano that is not quite at concert pitch.
